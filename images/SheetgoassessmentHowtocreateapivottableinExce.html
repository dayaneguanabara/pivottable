How to create a pivot table in Excel

Analyzing information is an essential part of any business, but it can be overwhelming to know where to start with so many resources out there. "Should I invest in sophisticated software?" "How can I simplify this task?"

 

There is no need to make a huge investment. Use a widely available tool: spreadsheets are a great way to convert your data into profound insights, whether in the company you work or in your own business.

In this easy to follow tutorial, you will learn how to create your pivot table from scratch in Excel and analyze data much faster.

Why should I use Excel’s pivot tables?

Pivot tables are one of Excel's most powerful features, as they allow you to handle large amounts of data and find patterns with just a few clicks. They help to extract meaning from the endless confusion of numbers on the screen.

For example, you can summarize data into smaller, more manageable parts that everyone can quickly understand. The image below shows this in action by efficiently organizing data in product categories and subcategories to visualize better what sold the most and had the best profit margin.

Unlike a static report, a pivot table allows you to explore a large set of data interactively to see it from many different perspectives in real-time, without the need to change the original file. And you don't even need formulas!

Basic steps for creating a pivot table in Excel:

To understand pivot tables practically, let's use a dataset that every company has: a customer base. We will find out who are the most valuable clients and those non-performing ones to outline an action plan appropriate for each situation, and much more.

  1. Choose your data source and double-check it's formatted right.

Open Excel and select your spreadsheet file by clicking File and Open. Next, verify the data is formatted in a way compatible with the pivot tables tool, as described:

  • Organize it in a tabular format, with columns that have headings.
  • Make sure you don’t have a whole row or an entire column of empty cells.
  • The data types in a column must be consistent. For example, don't mix dates and text.

  1. Create a pivot table in Excel.

Click on any cell in your data and go to the Insert tab. Once there, press on the PivotTable icon, as shown below.

When the Create PivotTable window appears, notice the data range is already filled in, and the location where the pivot table will open is set to New Worksheet. You can change this manually, but the default options work in most cases.

Click OK, and a new spreadsheet will open with a blank pivot table in it. The fun part is about to begin!

  1. Bring data to life by customizing the pivot table.

Before we start analyzing the data, let's do a little overview:

  • To the left of the spreadsheet is the empty PivotTable, where your report will appear as soon as we start working on the fields.
  • On the right, you will find the Fields (a list of your column’s headings) and four areas (Columns, Rows, Values, Filters) where you can place them by dragging and dropping.

Now is time to decide what information you want to extract from the summary. For example, in this tutorial, we will find out who are the biggest buyers in this customer base. Begin by dragging Customer_name from fields to the Rows area and then Profit into the Values section.

After this, we will organize the data in descending order to highlight the difference between customers. Click on Row Labels and change Sort by from Customer_name to Sum of Profit.

That's it, in only a few steps, you were able to view the profit by customers, starting with those that brought the lowest return.

Let's continue exploring the possibilities by adding Product_category and Product_subcategory in the Rows area, above Customer_name, and subsequently drag the Quantity field to the Value section.

As you can see, we have created a pivot table that shows the number of products sold and their profit, divided by category, subcategory, and customers.

It's a quick and efficient way to visualize your data, and that's one of the reasons why this feature is a favorite for data analysis in Excel.
